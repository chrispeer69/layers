How to Recognize and Face the Unresolved Layers in Your Marriage

Christopher Peer
The Book

A marriage doesn't unravel in one moment. It unravels in layers.

Every unspoken hurt. Every avoided conversation. Every apology never quite given. Every borrowed definition of love that two people brought into a marriage without knowing it. These things do not disappear — they compress. They become the invisible weight that over time suffocates the love that once felt effortless.

Layers is for every couple who has ever sat across the dinner table feeling miles apart and not understanding how they got there. For the husband who doesn't know how to say I'm sorry and mean it. For the wife who has said it so many times she's lost count. For every family quietly wondering if it is possible to find their way back.

The answer, for most couples willing to do the work, is yes.

The layers can be peeled back. Two people who have spent years building walls between themselves can begin the slow, sacred work of tearing those walls down.

The Foundation

The Five Layers Every Marriage Carries

They form the way sediment forms — gradually, invisibly, through the ordinary accumulation of moments we did not fully address.

01
The Unspoken Thing

The grievance filed away because saying it felt too risky, too small, or too late. The first layer — and the one that makes all others possible.

02
The Pattern of Avoidance

When avoidance becomes the default mode of a marriage. Subjects steered around so consistently they feel like walls.

03
The Scorecard

The invisible accounting of who gave more, who sacrificed more, who apologized last. Most couples deny keeping score. Almost all do.

04
The Borrowed Story

The inherited definition of love — written by the marriage you watched growing up. Two people. Two entirely different languages of love.

05
The Pre-Marital Wound

What you brought into the marriage from before it. The fears, wounds, and beliefs that play out as if they were always about the marriage.

The Fight That Saved Our Marriage — And What We Wish We'd Known Before It

By Christopher & Lisa Peer  ·  March 2025  ·  8 min read

We have been married long enough to know what an argument that matters feels like — and what one that doesn't looks like dressed up like one that does. For years, we fought the wrong fights. We argued about the dishes, about schedules, about money, about the kids. We argued about things that were never the real issue because the real issue was too raw, too central, too much of a risk to put on the table.

The real issue, as best as we can name it looking back, was this: we were both afraid the other one didn't see us. Not the surface version of us — not the person doing the jobs and playing the roles. The actual us. The person underneath who was scared and trying hard and sometimes failing and desperately needing to know that none of that made us less loveable to the person across the table.

We didn't know how to say that. So instead, we said: Why don't you ever help with this? Why does your work always come first? Why do I feel like I'm doing this alone?

What we were really saying was: do I still matter to you? Are you still paying attention? Because lately I feel like I'm invisible, and I don't know what to do with that.

The Night It Finally Broke Open

There was one argument — we don't remember exactly when, and it doesn't matter exactly when — that was different from the others. Not louder. Not more dramatic. In some ways, it was quieter than most. But it was the one where both of us, for some reason neither of us can fully explain, said the thing under the thing instead of the thing on top.

Lisa said: I don't need you to fix this. I need you to stay in the room. I need you to not go somewhere else inside your head the moment it gets uncomfortable. I need you to be present for what is actually happening between us.

And Christopher said — and this was harder, cost more, required bypassing a lifetime of trained defensiveness: I hear you. And you're right. And I don't know how to do what you're describing, but I want to learn. I'm afraid that if I sit still in the hard stuff, you'll see that I don't have answers. That I'm not as capable as I look.

Something happened in that moment. Not a resolution — resolutions don't come that fast. But a recognition. We saw each other, briefly, in the place underneath the armor. And what we saw there was not frightening. It was just human. It was just two people who loved each other and were both carrying things alone that were always meant to be carried together.

What We Wish We Had Known Sooner

We wish someone had told us early in our marriage that the argument you are having is almost never the argument you are actually having. That when you find yourself fighting about the same thing over and over, it is because the surface issue is not the source. The source is something deeper. Something that has a name, if you are willing to sit still long enough to find it.

We wish someone had told us that being known — truly known, in the places where you are least presentable — is not a vulnerability that weakens a marriage. It is the whole point. You cannot build a real marriage on the curated version of yourself. You can only build it on the truth of what you actually are, offered with courage to the person who chose you.

We wish someone had told us that the fight that feels like it might end everything can sometimes be the fight that finally begins something real. That the moment when you are both sitting in the wreckage of a conversation that went wrong and wondering what you're doing — that moment is not the end. It might be the exact beginning.

What You Can Do Today

If you and your spouse are in a season of fighting the wrong fights, here is the one thing we would ask you to try: the next time an argument starts, pause. Before you say the thing you were about to say, ask yourself: is this the real thing, or is this the surface version of the real thing? What am I actually trying to say right now? What am I actually afraid of?

You don't have to say all of that out loud immediately. But if you can name it to yourself first, you give yourself the possibility of saying the true thing — and the true thing, spoken honestly to the person you love, has a way of landing differently than the surface version ever can.

The fight that saved our marriage didn't look like a turning point while it was happening. It looked like two tired people finally telling the truth. That is all. That is everything.

— Christopher & Lisa Peer
